Climate Change: Science, Impacts, and International Response

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) was established in 1988 by the United Nations Environment Programme and the World Meteorological Organization. The IPCC assesses scientific literature on climate change and produces comprehensive assessment reports. Hoesung Lee has served as Chair of the IPCC. The IPCC's Sixth Assessment Report, published between 2021 and 2022, concluded with high confidence that human activities have caused approximately 1.1°C of global warming since the pre-industrial period.

Carbon dioxide is the primary greenhouse gas responsible for anthropogenic climate change. The concentration of CO2 in the atmosphere has risen from approximately 280 parts per million before industrialisation to over 420 parts per million in 2023. The burning of fossil fuels, including coal, oil, and natural gas, is the largest source of CO2 emissions. Deforestation is the second largest contributor to atmospheric CO2 increases.

The Paris Agreement was adopted in December 2015 at COP21 in Paris, France. The agreement was negotiated under the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change. The Paris Agreement aims to limit global warming to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and to pursue efforts to limit warming to 1.5°C. The United States, under President Barack Obama, was a key supporter of the agreement. The Trump administration withdrew the United States from the Paris Agreement in 2020, but President Joe Biden rejoined the agreement on his first day in office in January 2021.

The European Union has established a Green Deal, led by Commission President Ursula von der Leyen, with the goal of making Europe the first climate-neutral continent by 2050. The EU Emissions Trading System is one of the world's largest carbon markets. Germany has committed to phasing out coal by 2038 and has invested heavily in renewable energy through its Energiewende programme.

China is currently the world's largest emitter of greenhouse gases, responsible for approximately 27% of global CO2 emissions. Chinese President Xi Jinping pledged in 2020 that China would achieve carbon neutrality by 2060. China is also the world's largest investor in renewable energy, particularly solar panels and wind turbines.

Renewable energy technologies have experienced dramatic cost reductions. Solar photovoltaic power generation costs have fallen by over 90% since 2010. Wind energy has similarly become cost-competitive with fossil fuels in many regions. The International Energy Agency, led by Fatih Birol, projects that renewable energy will account for the majority of new electricity generation capacity globally.

The Arctic is warming approximately four times faster than the global average, a phenomenon known as Arctic amplification. Sea ice extent has declined dramatically, with the Arctic Ocean expected to experience ice-free summers before 2050. Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets are losing mass at accelerating rates, contributing to sea level rise. Rising sea levels threaten coastal communities worldwide, particularly small island developing states.

Scientists including James Hansen, formerly of NASA's Goddard Institute for Space Studies, have been warning about the dangers of climate change since the 1980s. Hansen testified before the United States Congress in 1988, marking one of the first high-profile political moments for climate science. Michael Mann developed the "hockey stick" graph showing rapid temperature increases in the 20th century.

The concept of net-zero emissions refers to achieving a balance between greenhouse gas emissions produced and those removed from the atmosphere. Many countries and corporations have set net-zero targets for 2050. Carbon capture and storage technologies are being developed to remove CO2 from industrial processes. Nature-based solutions, including forest preservation and restoration, are also being pursued as complementary strategies.
